The Trench and the Travelers

Overview

Beneath the cliffs of Big Sur lies a hidden world — a submarine canyon deeper than the Grand Canyon itself. In its cold, eternal blue, whales pass like ghosts through shadow and light, tracing routes older than memory. From the winding highways and cliffside homes of the South Coast, people watch these migrations unfold in real time — humpbacks breaching through fog, gray whales rolling close to the rocks, and the rare shimmer of a blue whale far offshore. This site invites you beneath the surface into the deepwater trench, along the path of the giants who travel it, and into the pulse of an ecosystem both fragile and magnificent.

What You'll Find on This Site

  • Seasonal patterns and directions of travel for key species
  • A migration table for shore-based watchers

When to Look

Peak activity varies by species. For example, humpbacks often feed here between late spring and fall, while gray whales are most visible during their winter through spring migrations.

Visit NOAA Fisheries and MBARI for research, maps, and current information.

Watching from Shore

Quick Tips & FAQs

Best places to watch?

Headlands, highway turnouts, and safe coastal pullouts with a wide view of the trench edge.

What behavior should I look for?

Look for blows first, then tail slaps, breaches (humpbacks), and slow rolling (grays) near shore.

Safety & etiquette?

Stay well back from cliff edges, obey signage, and pack out everything you bring.
