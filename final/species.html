Overview

The Migratory Giants of Big Sur

The Big Sur coastline is one of the world’s premier whale-viewing regions, thanks to the Monterey Submarine Canyon—a deepwater trench that draws nutrient-rich waters close to shore. Each year, several whale species pass by on ancient routes of feeding and breeding. Some linger for months, while others move through quickly, offering lucky observers a glimpse of ocean life at its most powerful and graceful.

Meet the Species

Humpback Whales

Famous for breaching and tail-slapping, humpbacks are the acrobats of Big Sur’s waters. They migrate here to feed on schools of anchovies and krill between April and December. Their long pectoral fins and patterned tails make them easy to identify from shore.

Gray Whales

Gray whales make the longest known migration of any mammal—nearly 10,000 miles round-trip between the Arctic and Baja. They travel close to the cliffs of Big Sur, especially during the northbound return when mothers guide calves near shore.

Blue Whales

The largest animals on Earth, blue whales can reach 100 feet long and weigh nearly 200 U.S. tons. They visit the central coast from May through October, following dense swarms of krill near the canyon’s edge.

Orcas (Killer Whales)

Intelligent, fast, and social, orcas appear off Big Sur mainly in spring and fall. Transient pods occasionally pass close to shore while hunting seals, sea lions, or dolphins. Their tall dorsal fins and black-and-white markings make them unmistakable.
