Overview

About Gray Whales

Gray whales (Eschrichtius robustus) complete one of the longest mammal migrations, traveling between Arctic feeding grounds and Baja California breeding lagoons. The Big Sur coastline offers reliable, land-based viewing because gray whales often hug the cliffs, especially mothers with calves during the northbound journey in late winter and spring. Watch for slow rolling near kelp beds, low bushy blows, and tail flukes when they dive on deeper segments of the route.

Key Near-Shore Clues

  • Close, steady travel parallel to the coastline
  • Calves surfacing frequently beside larger adults
  • Visible mottled gray skin and barnacle patches in calm seas

Migration Patterns

Southbound and Northbound Seasons

Southbound travel typically occurs from December to February as whales head to warm breeding lagoons. Northbound migration follows from February to April, with mothers and calves often hugging the coastline more tightly for protection. For background and identification tips, see NOAA — Gray Whale.
